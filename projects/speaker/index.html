Integrated Speaker
System Design

End-to-end design of a near-production speaker system — custom woofer and tweeter with SolidWorks-modeled magnetic motor structure and voice coil, a golden-ratio ash-wood enclosure to suppress standing-wave modes, and a full LTSpice-simulated signal chain including two three-band equalizer variants and a passive three-way crossover network.

Scope Mechanical + Electronics
CAD SolidWorks
Simulation LTSpice
Status Near-production
// 01 · overview
Project Summary

The project called for designing a complete speaker system from first principles — not just selecting off-the-shelf components, but engineering custom driver units with full mechanical and electromagnetic detail, an acoustically tuned enclosure, and an active/passive signal processing chain. Every component was designed to meet real-world manufacturability constraints with 1 mm tolerance allowances throughout.

Two equalizer approaches were designed and optimized in parallel: a passive RLC-based design and an active op-amp design. Both were simulated in LTSpice and iteratively improved, achieving peak flatness improvements from 1.6 dB to 0.6 dB (passive) and 3.5 dB to 1.05 dB (active). A three-way passive crossover network completed the signal chain, routing bass to the woofer, midrange through the equalizers, and highs to the tweeter with −3 dB crossover points at 350 Hz and 2500 Hz.

// 02 · contributions
What I Built
// mechanical
Woofer Driver Design
Designed the woofer from scratch in SolidWorks — magnetic motor structure, voice coil geometry, spider suspension, and cone assembly. Material selections balanced stiffness, damping coefficient, thermal tolerance, and cost with full GD&T annotations.
// mechanical
Tweeter Driver Design
Engineered the tweeter with a dome diaphragm and compact ferrofluid-cooled voice coil assembly. Designed to reproduce high-frequency content above the 2500 Hz crossover point with minimal resonance.
// acoustics
Enclosure Design
Designed the ash-wood enclosure using golden-ratio cavity proportions (262×235×634 mm) to suppress standing-wave modes. Modeled in SolidWorks with internal bracing, driver cutouts, and flush-mount hardware.
// electronics
Passive 3-Band Equalizer
Designed and simulated a passive three-band equalizer using RLC bandpass, RC lowpass, and RC highpass filters with a summing amplifier. Optimized component values in LTSpice to reduce peak-to-valley variation from 1.6 dB to 0.6 dB.
// electronics
Active 3-Band Equalizer
Designed an active op-amp equalizer with a 2nd-order active RC bandpass, active lowpass, and active highpass filter topology. Iterated on gain and cutoff values to reduce response variation from 3.5 dB to 1.05 dB — a 70% improvement.
// electronics
3-Way Passive Crossover
Designed a three-way passive crossover network with −3 dB crossover frequencies at 350 Hz and 2500 Hz, routing bass, midrange, and treble to their respective drivers. Verified crossover band overlap in LTSpice AC sweep.

design note
The enclosure cavity dimensions were derived from the golden ratio (≈1:1.618) applied across all three axes. This proportioning prevents two or more standing-wave modes from coinciding at the same frequency — a key acoustic design principle for reducing resonance coloration. Ash wood was selected over MDF and birch after a multi-criteria materials analysis weighing stiffness, internal damping, machinability, density, and cost.

// 05 · simulation results
Frequency Response Analysis

All frequency responses were verified via LTSpice AC sweep simulation (10 Hz – 10 kHz). The crossover network shows clean −3 dB handoffs at 350 Hz and 2500 Hz with acceptable band overlap. Both equalizers were iteratively optimized: the passive design improved peak-to-valley variation by 63%, while the active design improved by 70%.
